Senior Capstone Project Presentation

University of Alaska Anchorage, Spring 2021

Ty Bergstrom & Ian McAndrews

We developed an app for the Alaska Public Health Information Response Team to help monitor and respond to misinformation in social media. The app significantly simplifies their notification process. We also used the app as an opportunity to provide a variety of Machine Learning and Natural Language Processing tools and data visualizations to help their efforts.





The App

Submit Comments

Students who monitor social media for misinformation can easily log in and copy+paste the link to a comment along with the text.

Get Notifications

The response team receives an automatic email with a link that takes them directly to the comment so they can quickly respond.


View Comments

The Response Team members can also log in and quickly view the newest comments. It's more accessible to view which comments they need to prepare responses for, and the links take them directly to the comments.

Security

Only registered users have access to the app, and only the admin account can invite new users. There is no software to download to use the app.


Admin

The admin account has control of managing, inviting, and removing users. It's also easy to add responders to the email-only list, so they don't have to use the app at all. This makes it easier for more members to join the team and be involved.

Users

The student-monitor users have access to the app to submit comments, and responders can also use the app for the data visuals. Responders on the email-only list will not have access.


The ML and NLP Projects

Data Exploration

The app is also an interface for generating data visualizations with different ML and NLP tools


WordClouds

WordClouds are a great way of visualizing the most frequent and important words in a text. It's easy to generate them for comments over different date ranges and compare the differences.

WordClouds

This is an example of processing the text data to get the bigrams, or combinations of two words, which adds more information compared to only single words.


Most Frequent Words

Another way of visualizing frequent words is just with a bar plot.

Most Frequent Bigrams

One of the best ways to use these plots is to compare different time ranges to see how they change.


Topic Modelling with LDA

Topic modelling is another great NLP project. The interface lets users choose to generate either 4 or 6 topics, and how many words to display per topic.

Topic Modelling with LDA

The topic WordClouds are clusters of the words most strongly associated with each topic, and it takes some practice to analyze the results, but it's a great way to track the trending misinformation topics.


Sentiment Analysis

Sentiment analysis is also a great NLP project. We take the average sentiment score of all misinformation comments per day, and plot how it changes over time.

Sentiment Analysis

Sentiment scores below zero are negative sentiment, and above zero are postive.


Sentiment Analysis of Words

A similar idea we had was to plot the sentiment score of only comments containing a specific word.

Sentiment Analysis of Words

It's very interesting to see how some words are used in comments with more negative scores than others.


Track Word Usage

It's very helpful to see how frequently some words are used in misinformation comments over time. You can get an idea of when some trendy words come and go.

Sentiment Analysis of Words

It's fun to try plotting different words together to see whether they are used together. You can choose different colors with the interface.


Comments per Day

Another visualization that we can make with the dataset is a plot of how many misinformation comments were made per day.

Comments per Day of the Week

It helps the team to see this broken down by days of the week.
