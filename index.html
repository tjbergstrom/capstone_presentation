Senior Capstone Project Presentation

University of Alaska Anchorage, Spring 2021

Ty Bergstrom & Ian McAndrews

We developed an app for the Alaska Public Health Information Response Team to help monitor and respond to misinformation in social media. The app significantly simplifies their notification process. We also used the app as an opportunity to provide a variety of Machine Learning and Natural Language Processing tools and data visualizations to help their efforts.

Read more about the Response Team here




The App

Submit Comments

The students who monitor social media for misinformation can easily log in and copy+paste the link to a comment along with the text. Optional notes aid communication between monitors and responders.

Get Notifications

The Response Team receives an automatic email with a link that takes them directly to the comment so they can quickly respond.


View Comments

The Response Team members can also log in and quickly view the newest comments, along with the links.

Security

Only registered users have access to the app, and only the admin account can invite new users. There is no software to download to use the app.


Admin

The admin account has control of managing, inviting, and removing users. It's also easy to add responders to the email-only list, so they don't have to use the app at all. This makes it easier for more members to join the team and be involved.

Users

The student-monitor users have access to the app to submit comments, and responders can also use the app for the data visuals and to view previous comments. Responders on the email-only list will get the automatic notifications for new comments, but will not have access to the app.


The ML and NLP Projects

Data Exploration

The app is also an interface for generating data visualizations with different ML and NLP tools. The data is based on all of the misinformation comments submitted to the app.


WordClouds

WordClouds are a great way of visualizing the most frequent and important words in a text. It's easy to generate WordClouds of misinformation comments over different date ranges and compare the differences.

WordClouds

This one is an example of processing the text data to get the bigrams, or combinations of two words, which adds more information compared to only single words.


Most Frequent Words

Another way of visualizing frequent words in misinformation comments is just with a bar plot.

Most Frequent Bigrams

One of the best ways to use these plots is to compare different time ranges to see how they change.


Topic Modelling with LDA

Topic modelling is a great NLP project. The interface lets users choose to generate either 4 or 6 topics, and how many words to display per topic.

Topic Modelling with LDA

The topic WordClouds are clusters of the words most strongly associated with each topic. It takes some practice to analyze the results, and it's a great way to help track the trending topics in misinformation comments.


Sentiment Analysis

Sentiment analysis is another great NLP project. This takes the average sentiment score of all misinformation comments per day to show how it changes over time.

Sentiment Analysis

Sentiment scores below zero are negative, and above zero are postive.


Sentiment Analysis of Comments Containing a Word

A similar idea is to plot the sentiment score of only comments containing a specific word.

Sentiment Analysis of Comments Containing a Word

It's very interesting to see how some words are used in comments with more negative scores than others. Here, 'vaccine' is actually not as negative as others that we've seen.


Track Word Usage

It's helpful to see how frequently some words are used in misinformation comments over time. You can get an idea of when some trendy words come and go, while other words are always there.

Track Word Usage

It's fun to try plotting different words together to see how often they appear together. You can choose different colors with the interface.

Comments per Day

Another visualization that can be made with the available dataset is just to plot how many misinformation comments were made per day.

Comments per Day of the Week

It helps the team to see this broken down by days of the week. Here, it appears they need to be ready to respond more on Wednesdays.


List of Unique Posts

This list is a way of quickly seeing which original posts had misinformation comments, and how many comments were made on each post.


Data Collection

There are lots of other older misinformation comments on the monitored pages, and those can be collected to enhance the dataset without sending a notification the Response Team. This interface makes it very easy to collect data, compared to entering everything in a spreadsheet.

Searchable Database

All of the misinformation comments that have been collected can be displayed, and it's easy to use CTRL+F in your browser to search through the database.
